Lab 3 – Buy vs Rent (10-Year Outlook)

Note

Watch Maya’s 10-second introduction before you compare the options.

Maya knows Austin housing is pricey, but she also knows staying on the sidelines for too long can be costly. Your job in Lab 3 is to use the spreadsheet template from Lab 2 as a decision tool that answers two core questions:

  1. Can Maya afford to buy a home today on her $85 000 salary—while still contributing to retirement and paying down debt?
  2. If not, what salary (or trade-offs) would she need to make buying feasible?

Bonus (+ 20 pts): At a feasible salary, which path leaves her wealthiest after 10 years—buying one of the three properties or continuing to rent? This requires running the Buy vs Rent models and accounting for different financing types and the retirement opportunity cost.

You will compare four realistic paths:

  • Buying the Barton-Springs condo a stone’s throw from work
  • Buying a smaller starter home on Lightfoot Trail
  • Buying a lower-priced starter in Kyle, TX, 22 miles away
  • Continuing to rent a two-bed South Lamar apartment with a roommate

At the end you will recommend a path and explain the trade-offs of affordability, down-payment size, PMI vs FHA mortgage insurance, retirement contributions, and lifestyle factors like commute and HOA dues.

2 The three questions you must answer (in order)

Phase 1 — Affordability gate
Can Maya afford to buy any of these homes today?

Where to work: Start on the input tab — confirm Maya's salary ($85 000), retirement contribution rate at 17 % (cell D75), debt balances, and living expenses are loaded correctly. Keep the 17 % retirement contribution fixed throughout Phase 1.

Step 1 — Quantify the rent cost. On the 5 year Budgeting tab, set the toggle in cell E18 to 1 (renting mode — row 20 will pull Maya's share of rent from input cell D30). Look at Maya's Savings line (row 47). This tells you how much cash she has left each month after rent and all other expenses.

Step 2 — Quantify each buy option's cost. For each of the three properties, enter its details on the input tab (Buying section: home price, HOA, property-tax rate, utilities, insurance, etc.). Ignore the down payment for now. Then use the financing dropdown (cell K5) to cycle through all four financing options: Conventional High-Down, Conventional Low-Down + PMI, FHA High-Down + MIP, and FHA Low-Down + MIP. For each combination, open the Buying Model tab and record the implied monthly housing expense (row 25 pre-tax, or row 42 after-tax benefit).

You should end up with 12 monthly costs (3 properties × 4 financing types) plus the rent cost.

Step 3 — Test affordability. For each of those 12 scenarios and the rent option, apply the standard lending guidelines:

  • 28 % rule — housing expense should not exceed 28 % of gross monthly income
  • 36 % rule — housing + all debt payments should not exceed 36 % of gross monthly income

In your memo: Present a table of monthly housing costs for all 12 buy scenarios and rent. Report the 28 % and 36 % ratios for each. Which options (if any) pass? What does this tell you about Maya's readiness to buy at her current salary?

Phase 2A — What salary would make buying feasible?
Find the salary Maya needs to afford each buy option.

Where to work: Your input tab should already have the property details from Phase 1. Assume for now that Maya has the down payment available. Pick the buy option and financing type you want to test.

Go to the 5 year Budgeting (rent or buy) tab and set the toggle in cell E18 to 0 (buying mode — this lets you input the mortgage cost from the Buying Model into row 20; ignore any double-accounting for utilities for now). Check whether Maya's Savings line (row 47) stays positive — if it goes deeply negative, she cannot sustain that option at her current salary.

Adjust the salary cell (D7 on the input tab) upward until the budget works. Find two salary levels (recall: the 28 % rule caps housing expense at 28 % of gross monthly income, and the 36 % rule caps housing + all debt at 36 %):

  • Break-even salary — the salary where Savings (row 47) just reaches zero. Report the 28/36 % ratios at this salary. They will likely be well above the guidelines — discuss what risks that poses (no emergency cushion, one unexpected expense puts Maya underwater).
  • Safe salary — the salary where the 28/36 % ratios are actually respected. This is the income level a lender would realistically approve.

Test both salary levels under at least two retirement scenarios:

  • Keeping the 17 % retirement contribution (cell D75)
  • Reducing retirement to the employer-match-only level (e.g., 3–6 %) — change cell D75 and observe how the Savings line and the required salary shift

In your memo: Present both the break-even and safe salaries for your chosen options under both retirement scenarios. Report the 28/36 % ratios at each salary level. Discuss: what does Maya give up in long-term retirement wealth if she cuts contributions to afford a home sooner?

Phase 2B — How long to save the down payment?
At a salary where the 28/36 % ratios are respected, how long must Maya rent before she can afford the down payment?

Where to work: Go back to the renting scenario — set cell E18 to 1 on the 5 year Budgeting tab. Set the salary on the input tab to the safe salary from Phase 2A (the one where 28/36 % is respected). Keep whichever retirement contribution rate you are testing.

Look at cumulative savings (row 49) and investment balance (row 59) over time. Find how many years it takes Maya to accumulate enough for the required down payment plus closing costs. Check at least two financing paths: 25 % conventional down and 4 % FHA down on your target property.

In your memo: Report the down-payment timeline for each financing type. Does FHA's lower down payment meaningfully shorten the wait? What is the trade-off between a faster purchase (FHA, lower DP) and the ongoing MIP cost over 30 years?

Bonus — Phase 3: Buy vs. Rent net-worth comparison (+20 pts)
At a feasible salary, which path leaves Maya wealthiest after 10 years?

This phase is optional bonus credit. Complete Phases 1 and 2 first.

Where to work: Your input tab should already have the feasible salary and property details from Phase 2. Now use the Buying Model and Renting Model tabs to read the 10-year net-worth projections. Repeat for each buy option (change the property inputs each time) and record the results.

Financing type matters here. The choice of Conventional 20 %, Conventional 5 % + PMI, or FHA 3.5 % + MIP significantly changes the monthly cost, the equity build-up, and therefore the 10-year net-worth outcome. Test at least two financing scenarios per property and compare.

Retirement caveat: The Buying and Renting models assume retirement contributions stay the same across both paths. If Maya had to reduce her 401(k) rate to afford buying, run the analysis twice — once with the buyer's rate and once with the renter's rate — and add the retirement balance gap to the renter's net worth for a fair comparison.

In your memo: Name the best option and support it with numbers. Show how financing type (Conventional vs FHA, high vs low down payment) affects the outcome. Does the ranking change when you include the retirement opportunity cost?
